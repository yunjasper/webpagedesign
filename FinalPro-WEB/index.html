This is the final project of the course. To mark this moment, there are some differences as compared to the previous assignments:

  • You work in a team of 2 (not more than 3!) -- instead of working alone
  • You use an HTML editor (instead of Notepad)
  • You can choose the topic of the project (among the topics suggested below)

After you read the whole page and you have an idea on what topic you want to work on, get in touch with your partner who must have a DIFFERENT topic from yours. Once both of you (you and your partner) decide on your topics and you both agree to work together, you start working!

You will use an HTML editor of your choice. Of course, you'll make a search for such an editor. Some of them -- not all of them -- are free. One place to look for an HTML editor would be on Google; another place would be on Wikipedia.

If you must, you can also look for one that is on the Marianopolis' network...

Why HTML editor? Because once you learn how to use it (which shouldn't take too long) you can create webpages with a minimum amount of code typing. Why do I tell you now about such a software? Because from time to time you still need to type some code which will be easier now, with all the knowledge and experience accumulated in the previous assignments.

If you already have such a software and you want to use it - just do it! If not, look for one (see above)!

Note: Separately from the webpages that you create for this project, you have to submit a report on the HTML editor that you used (for details of the report, see What to Submit section of this page). If you and your partner used the same editor, you submit one single report; otherwise, one report is required for each editor used.

Once you decide on the HTML editor, you explore it and you use it to create and/or expand ONE of the following:


  1. A simple Tic-Tac-Toe game, using the colours red and blue instead of X and O. The "board" is a 3-by-3 frameset. In it, the initial content of each frame is a tiny webpage consisting of just two links, one to a mini-page with no content other that a red background colour, the other identical to this, except with blue background colour. Alternately, if you prefer, link to a graphic image consisting of all red/all blue respectively. If you choose this, try to fill the entire frame area with the chosen colour.
    The links themselves should stand out, and use your imagination in their design. At first (in the design stage) work with two simple textual links, and then replace them with small thumbnail sized images (red/blue) as links to the "real thing".
    Here is an example to help you.

    NOTE: You are not playing against the computer, that would require programming (it can be done, but goes way beyond the level of this course). You are designing the game for two human players who pass the mouse to each other.

  2. A non-latin alphabet , e.g. Arabic, Chinese, Cyrillic, Hebrew, Japanese, Korean, Tamil, ..., with a very short explanation of the phonetics, the direction of text (left-to-right, right-to-left, vertical, etc.), a copy of the alphabet itself and a few friendly examples for illustration, e.g. a nice "Hello", a peace message, etc. Of course, you are required to give a sample of the alphabet and an English translation of the messages, please!
    Use online information to find explanations on this topic. The information could be about Declaring Your Page's Character Encoding and Specifying Your Page's Language. The Character Encoding is declared in the <head> section, as the charset attribute to the <meta> tag.
    This is what the browser uses to recognize the alphabet you used. Do not waste time trying to create your message in a wordprocessor. Just fish it out on the Internet. Once you found a page you like, in the language of your choice, examine the source of that page to find the charset code.

  3. A magic square. It consists in a frameset of 3 by 3 and each cell offers the possibility to display a number from 1 to 9. The goal of this game is to obtain a total of 15 horizontally, vertically and diagonally - all in the same time! Each number has to be used only once (see example). At the bottom of the window, you have two links: one for a new game and the other to display a solution to the problem. In other words, more frames...
    Of course, you must have a link back to "final project" (see below) and you may consider an extra-link (on each 'big' number) for "correction".

    NOTE: You are not playing against the computer, that would require programming (it can be done, but goes way beyond the level of this course.) You are designing the game for one human player who uses your webpage as a gameboard.
